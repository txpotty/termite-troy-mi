Different termite species (e.g., subterranean, drywood, dampwood) require distinct treatment approaches. Our experts are highly skilled in identifying the specific termite species infesting your property. This crucial step ensures that the subsequent treatment plan is precisely calibrated for maximum efficacy against the identified pest, preventing misapplication of resources and guaranteeing powerful results.

Customized Treatment Plans

Targeted Subterranean Termite Treatment

Subterranean termites are the most common and destructive type in Michigan. Our primary strategy typically involves creating an impenetrable treatment zone around your home’s foundation using advanced liquid termiticides. These products are carefully applied into the soil, creating a barrier that termites cannot bypass. When termites attempt to cross this treated zone, they are either repelled or eliminated. We also utilize baiting systems, which involve strategically placing bait stations around your property. Termites feed on the bait, transfer the active ingredient to other colony members, leading to a domino effect that eliminates the entire colony, including the queen. This method provides long-term protection and is particularly effective where liquid treatments may be challenging.

Drywood Termite Solutions

While less common in Michigan, drywood termites can pose a threat. For these pests, which live entirely within wood, localized treatments such as fumigation, direct wood injection, or heat treatments may be employed. Fumigation, for instance, involves tenting the entire structure and introducing a gas that penetrates all wood members, effectively eliminating the infestation. Direct wood injection targets specific infested areas, minimizing disruption, while heat treatments raise the temperature of the infested wood to lethal levels for termites.

Advanced Treatment Technologies

Non-Repellent Termiticides

Our preferred liquid treatments utilize non-repellent termiticides, which are undetectable to termites. Unlike older repellent products that termites could avoid, non-repellents allow termites to unknowingly come into contact with the treated zone. As they tunnel through the soil, they pick up the termiticide and transfer it to other termites through their grooming habits and food sharing, leading to a more widespread and complete colony elimination. This ‘Trojan horse’ effect is incredibly powerful in eradicating entire colonies.

Baiting Systems for Continuous Monitoring

Termite baiting systems not only eliminate existing colonies but also serve as a continuous monitoring tool. Placed strategically around your property, these stations allow us to detect new termite activity before it reaches your home, providing an early warning system. This proactive approach ensures ongoing protection and rapid intervention should new threats emerge, preventing future infestations from taking hold, ensuring continuous peace of mind.

Common Indicators of Termite Activity

Discarded Wings

One of the most common and earliest signs of a termite infestation, specifically for subterranean termites, is the presence of discarded wings. After swarming (their reproductive flight), termites shed their wings, often found near windows, doors, or other light sources. These wings resemble small fish scales and are a clear indication that a termite colony is attempting to establish itself nearby. The presence of these wings suggests significant activity, even if you don’t see the termites themselves. This is a critical early warning sign that should prompt immediate investigation, as it signifies that a mature colony is breeding and expanding, potentially into your home. The sheer quantity of discarded wings can be alarming and points to a large-scale reproductive event.

Mud Tubes

Subterranean termites build distinctive mud tubes (also known as ‘shelter tubes’) for protection as they travel between their underground colony and their food source (your home’s wooden structures). These pencil-sized tubes are made of soil, wood, and termite saliva, and are commonly found on foundations, walls, floor joists, or even inside your basement or crawl space. They are a definitive sign of active subterranean termite presence and indicate that termites are actively foraging for wood within your property. Breaking open a tube can reveal live termites. The location and density of these tubes can indicate the pathways termites are using to access your home, offering direct clues for targeted treatment. Their presence signifies an active, ongoing infestation where termites are actively consuming wood within your structure, silently causing damage.

Damaged Wood

While often hidden, damaged wood is a tell-tale sign of termite activity. This can manifest as wood that sounds hollow when tapped, inexplicable buckling of wooden floors or ceilings, or maze-like patterns within walls or furniture. Termites consume wood from the inside out, leaving a thin veneer that often appears normal from the exterior. This kind of damage can progress silently for years, compromising the structural integrity of your property before becoming outwardly visible. Any soft spots, unexplained holes, or crumbling wood should raise immediate suspicion. Often, the damage is internal, making a professional inspection crucial to uncover hidden issues. The extent of this damage can be staggering, leading to costly repairs if not addressed promptly, highlighting the destructive nature of these hidden pests. Discovering wood that appears like it has been eaten through, resembling honeycomb, is a definitive indicator of an advanced infestation requiring immediate attention.

Frass (Termite Droppings)

For drywood termites, a common sign is the appearance of ‘frass’—tiny, pellet-like droppings that resemble sawdust or coffee grounds. These are usually found near infested wood, often in small piles on floors or windowsills. Unlike subterranean termites, drywood termites do not need soil contact and push their fecal pellets out of existing wood, creating these tell-tale piles.
